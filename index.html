Five days at NHL Stenden, exploring cybersecurity, AI and Dutch culture, as experienced by students from Howest Hogeschool in Bruges.

Monday · 9 March 2026

From Bruges to Friesland

A long train ride north, and a first glimpse of a city we'd never visited before.

Our journey started at Bruges station. A train across the flat, windmill-dotted Dutch landscape brought us to Leeuwarden by early evening. The capital of Friesland and, as we quickly discovered, a city full of quiet surprises.

We checked into our accommodation, took a short walk around the city centre and called it an early night. Tomorrow, the real programme would begin.

Tuesday · 10 March 2026

Welcome to NHL Stenden

A full day of guest lectures, a campus tour, and an evening cooking dinner together in Leeuwarden.

Our first day at NHL Stenden started at 9 am. We were warmly welcomed with the hot coffee and tasty local Frisian desserts (oranjekoek). After a delightful welcome the first presentation began.

We learned how the programme was structured, what we would be doing each day, and got acquainted with programme leaders. The main topic was on internalisation. We discovered the different types of international students and how they adapt to study abroad and how it impacts them. The most entertaining part was associating yourself with one of the categories and recognizing true things about yourself.

Digital Security in Your Pocket

The second presentation revealed the sacred secrets of digital passports. Toby Zeldenrust explained the cryptography aspects behind the digital chips and shared curious facts about the international passports in general. It was fascinating to learn how authentication and digital signatures of passports actually work.

Lunch, Cooked by Students

At noon, we headed to lunch and what a surprise it was. The cantine of the NHL Stenden offered an impressive amount of dishes and what made it even more special is that all the meals were prepared by the students from the hotel management programme. To be honest the food was genuinely amazing! The true "repast"!

The third presentation came from ING Bank employees, who walked us through the secure development practices, deployment and general security practices used across the bank. We had no idea that ING bank was so spread around the world. There is a branch in Australia! Imagine how hard it is to co-work with such different time zones…

Afterwards, we went on a campus tour. We visited various facilities including the IT department of the NHL Stenden. The staff prepared some cookies for us and they were delicious! Unfortunately, the visit was brief as the department does not specialise in cyber security which is the field most of us are studying.

Security Governance & NIS2

The last but not least presentation covered security governance and strategies. NHL Stenden follows the security trends and shared their roadmap for implementing the NIS2 directive. That was a lot to take in, but it was the last presentation of the day.

We wrapped up around 5 pm and the evening was free. The students split into their own groups and went separate ways and so did we. Our group decided to go home and make a tasty dinner and have some time together, chatting about the first day and enjoying our time in Leeuwarden.

Wednesday · 11 March 2026

AI, Teams & the City

AI Experience Centre, a team workshop, intercultural session and a street art tour through Leeuwarden.

AI Experience Centre

Day 3 started around 10 AM. The participants gathered in front of the AI Center, which was located close to the main entrance of one of the campus buildings. After everyone arrived, the group was given a tour of the AI lab.

This part of the day was very interesting because it showed how artificial intelligence can be used in real-life situations.

The first thing that could be seen in the lab was a camera connected to a screen. It was analyzing people in real time and could estimate characteristics such as gender, emotion, and age. The system demonstrated how AI can process information from facial expressions almost instantly.

Next, there was a large touchscreen with a keyboard attached to it. The guides explained that it was an AI chatbot developed by NHL Stenden students. It was an interesting example of a project created by students using AI technology.

Another setup included a camera that periodically took photos of the people standing in front of it. Every one or two minutes, the AI generated a random theme and transformed the person's appearance. For example, it could show how someone might look in a different historical period or artistic style.

The lab also contained many 3D printed objects. One of the most memorable examples was a wavy chair designed to follow the shape of the human spine. It was surprisingly comfortable. There were also 3D printed tables, flowers, and other objects displayed around the room. Many of these objects were likely connected to the nearby 3D printing facilities.

Another demonstration involved a scanner that used machine learning to inspect objects. The example shown was a flower model. The AI had been trained using many examples of a perfect flower until it learned the correct pattern. After training, it could identify whether a flower was missing a leaf or had an unusual shape. The presenter explained that this type of system could be used for quality control in production environments.

An insect detection system was also presented, showing another practical use of AI technology.

Team Activities

After the AI lab tour, the group moved to one of the classrooms on the upper floors where several team activities took place.

The first activity was used to divide everyone into groups. Participants had to organize themselves according to their birth dates, starting with the year, then the month, and finally the day. Once everyone was in the correct order, they were divided into groups of six people.

The next activity was a series of competitive team games. Each group selected one representative who competed against a representative from another team.

One example involved food from different cuisines displayed on a screen. The instructor would say the name of a country, and the two representatives had to quickly point at the correct dish. Other rounds involved matching professions with objects and similar fast-reaction challenges.

The second major activity was a team challenge. Each group received a different problem to solve.

One group was asked to create the worst possible team. Instead of using famous people, the team created a group of people with conflicting personalities and opposite working styles. They then analyzed the problems that could appear in such a team and brainstormed possible solutions to improve cooperation and communication.

There was also a third activity. Every group received the same criminal case study and had to answer several questions about it.

What made this exercise interesting was that the case never explicitly identified who was responsible for the crime. Despite this, many participants immediately formed conclusions. Some believed the store owner was responsible because of the possibility of insurance money, while others suspected the cashier or another person involved in the story.

The purpose of the activity was to show how differently people can interpret the same information and how quickly assumptions can be made even when there is not enough evidence.

The activities finished around 12:30, and after that it was time for lunch.

Street Art City Tour

The afternoon tour through Leeuwarden's city centre turned out to be the biggest surprise of the week. The city is essentially an open-air gallery, with massive murals on every other wall, and tiny hidden figures tucked into the brickwork if you look closely enough.

Thursday · 12 March 2026

Cybersecurity & Berlin

A morning on risk management, the Odido hack, and a cross-border disaster project with NHL Stenden and HWR Berlin.

We started the morning of day 4 with our visit to the university. The first lecture we had was by two representatives of the cybersafety research group of NHL Stenden. We had discussions about risk management in a company, how a cyber attacker's mind works, why people without awareness training make so many human mistakes, and what the biggest weakness in a company is - which is humans. We had a lecture for about 1.5 hours, and once we were done, we continued with a small workshop.

The whole classroom was filled with software developers, business IT students, and us as cybersecurity students. They split us into 2 balanced groups, where one of the lecturers gave us an assignment to apply the Swiss cheese model on a specific hack that we chose: the ODIDO hack. ODIDO is a mobile phone company, just like Proximus. Apparently, in February there was a huge data breach affecting 6.5 million people's data. This happened because of a phishing attack: an attacker emailed an employee claiming they needed to do maintenance and therefore needed access to the system. The system was set up with multi-factor authentication. The employee, who lacked security awareness training, simply gave all the access, including the password and keys, to the attacker, who was pretending to be part of an IT audit.

The employee was just a random staff member, not an administrator or anything, yet had access to a system that only admins should have access to. Moreover, once the attacker got the data they needed, they demanded a ransom for it. For our task on the Swiss cheese model, we decided that security awareness training was the core component in this case. We then continued by adding data retention and minimization policies to reduce the attack surface, and role assignments to employees, since not every employee should have admin access. Since we were the only cybersecurity students in the group, we contributed a lot and shared our knowledge, which was our goal. After this, we had a quick lunch break.

When we went back to the classroom, we had the main finishing project to do at the end of the day. The point of this project was to choose a disaster that might happen in suburban cities, towns, or villages, and figure out how we would detect and mitigate it as fast as possible using existing patterns, tools, and AI. We started off by forming teams with NHL Stenden students and students in Berlin, which made us very diverse, ready to learn from each other and communicate.

We had two and a half hours to choose a disaster like a tsunami, set up a detection system, and use already existing resources such as weather apps and satellites. The security part was mostly about networks and connections, like switching protocols from HTTP to QUIC and making sure sniffing and altering network traffic wasn't possible. Once we finished, we reunited with the other groups and started presenting, which went well and was received positively.

At the end of the day, we said our thank yous and goodbyes and appreciated the opportunity we had to learn so much, both general and specific, in the computer science field.

Friday · 13 March 2026

The Yellow Train Home

Checkout, a last morning along the canals, and the long train back to Bruges.

📷 Train station

Friday was bittersweet. After checking out, we spent a last morning by the canals, old sailing ships moored along the waterfront, the city quiet and grey in the March light. Then the train station, the yellow NS carriages, and the long ride south.

The ride back to Bruges gave us time to reflect on everything: the lectures, the people, the ideas, and a city that surprised us at every turn.

What We Took Home

Looking back, the week left us with a few clear takeaways. Security is always layered, never a single wall. From passport chips to the Swiss cheese model, real protection comes from overlapping defences that catch what the others miss.

We also saw that AI is already in production, not just research. The AI lab showed real deployed systems, with computer vision working here and now. And working live with the Berlin students on the disaster project made it clear that international teams think better, since different perspectives lead to sharper outcomes.
