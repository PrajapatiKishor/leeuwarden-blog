A Week in Leeuwarden

Five days at NHL Stenden, exploring cybersecurity, AI and Dutch culture, as experienced by students from Howest Hogeschool in Bruges.

📍 Leeuwarden, Netherlands 🗓 March 9–13, 2026 🎓 NHL Stenden 🇧🇪 Howest Hogeschool, Bruges

Monday · 9 March 2026

From Bruges to Friesland

A long train ride north, and a first glimpse of a city we'd never visited before.

Our journey started at Bruges station. A train across the flat, windmill-dotted Dutch landscape brought us to Leeuwarden by early evening. The capital of Friesland and, as we quickly discovered, a city full of quiet surprises.

"Canals, historic sailing boats, old town charm, and a thick foggy atmosphere that made everything feel cinematic on arrival night."

We checked into our accommodation, took a short walk around the city centre and called it an early night. Tomorrow, the real programme would begin.

Tuesday · 10 March 2026

Welcome to NHL Stenden

A full day of guest lectures, a campus tour, and an evening cooking dinner together in Leeuwarden.

Our first day at NHL Stenden started at 9 am. We were warmly welcomed with hot coffee and tasty local Frisian desserts, oranjekoek, a sweet treat we hadn't come across before. A great way to start a day.

After the welcome, the first presentation began. We learned how the programme was structured, what we'd be doing each day, and got acquainted with the programme leaders. The main topic was internationalisation, covering the different types of international students, how they adapt to studying abroad, and how the experience shapes them. The most entertaining part was placing yourself into one of the categories and recognising truths you hadn't consciously thought about before.

Digital Security in Your Pocket

The second presentation revealed the sacred secrets of digital passports. Toby Zeldenrust explained the cryptography behind the digital chips and shared some genuinely curious facts about international passports. It was fascinating to learn how authentication and digital signatures actually work, a layer of technology most of us carry without ever thinking about.

"Your passport is one of the most sophisticated security devices you own, and it fits in your pocket."

Lunch, Cooked by Students

At noon, we headed to the canteen, and what a surprise it was. NHL Stenden offered an impressive range of dishes, and what made it even more special was that all the meals were prepared by students from the hotel management programme. The food was genuinely amazing. The true repast.

The third presentation came from ING Bank employees, who walked us through their secure development practices, deployment pipelines, and general security strategy. We had no idea how globally spread ING actually is. They have a branch in Australia. Imagine coordinating security across that many time zones.

Afterwards we went on a campus tour, visiting various facilities including the IT department. The staff had prepared cookies for the visit, delicious ones, as it happens. The tour was brief, as the department doesn't specialise in cybersecurity, but the campus itself was impressive: open, modern, and constantly buzzing.

Security Governance & NIS2

The last presentation of the day covered security governance and strategy. NHL Stenden shared their roadmap for implementing the NIS2 directive, covering risk management, supply chain security, board accountability, and security investments. A lot to take in, but a fitting way to close a packed day.

We wrapped up around 5 pm. The evening was free. Students split into their own groups and went their separate ways. Our group headed home, cooked a proper dinner together, and spent the evening chatting about the day and settling into life in Leeuwarden.

Wednesday · 11 March 2026

AI, Teams & the City

AI Experience Centre, a team workshop, intercultural session and a street art tour through Leeuwarden.

AI Experience Centre

Day 3 started around 10 AM. We gathered in front of the AI Center, located close to the main entrance of one of the campus buildings, and once everyone arrived we were given a tour of the AI lab. This was one of the most interesting parts of the whole week, because it showed how artificial intelligence actually gets used in real-life situations.

The first thing we saw was a camera connected to a screen, analysing people in real time and estimating characteristics like gender, emotion and age. It was striking how instantly the system could read information from facial expressions. Next to it was a large touchscreen with a keyboard, running an AI chatbot built by NHL Stenden students themselves, a nice example of students putting the technology to work.

Another setup used a camera that took photos every minute or two, then generated a random theme and transformed the person's appearance, showing how you might look in a different historical period or artistic style. The lab was also full of 3D printed objects: tables, flowers, and a wavy chair shaped to follow the human spine that turned out to be surprisingly comfortable. Most of these were tied to the nearby 3D printing facilities.

One demonstration that stuck with us was a scanner that used machine learning to inspect objects. The example was a flower model: the AI had been trained on many images of a perfect flower until it learned the correct pattern, and could then spot whether a flower was missing a leaf or had an unusual shape. The presenter explained this kind of system is used for quality control in production environments. An insect detection system rounded out the demos, another very practical use of the technology.

Team Activities

After the AI lab tour, we moved to a classroom on the upper floors for a series of team activities. The first one was just to split everyone up: we had to organise ourselves by birth date, first by year, then month, then day, before being divided into groups of six.

Then came a set of competitive team games. Each group picked a representative to go head-to-head against another team. One round showed dishes from different cuisines on a screen, the instructor called out a country, and the two representatives raced to point at the correct dish. Other rounds involved matching professions with objects and similar fast-reaction challenges.

The second major activity was a team challenge, with each group given a different problem. Our group was asked to build the worst possible team, not using famous people, but inventing a group with clashing personalities and opposite working styles, then analysing the problems that would arise and brainstorming ways to improve cooperation and communication.

There was also a third activity. Every group received the same criminal case study and had to answer several questions about it. What made it interesting was that the case never explicitly identified who was responsible for the crime. Despite this, many of us immediately formed conclusions. Some believed the store owner was responsible because of the possibility of insurance money, while others suspected the cashier or someone else in the story.

The purpose of the activity was to show how differently people can interpret the same information, and how quickly assumptions can be made even when there isn't enough evidence. The activities finished around 12:30, and after that it was time for lunch.

Street Art City Tour

The afternoon tour through Leeuwarden's city centre turned out to be the biggest surprise of the week. The city is essentially an open-air gallery, with massive murals on every other wall, and tiny hidden figures tucked into the brickwork if you look closely enough.

Thursday · 12 March 2026

Cybersecurity & Berlin

The Swiss Cheese Model, the Odido hack, and a live collaboration with students in Berlin.

📷 Swiss Cheese workshop
📷 Berlin online session

Thursday was the most intensive day. Eric de Bruin and Maarten te Kulve led a morning workshop on cybersafety using the Swiss Cheese Model, showing how security incidents happen when multiple protection layers fail simultaneously.

"We applied it to the Odido hack, with 0.5 million accounts exposed through a chain of small failures. Abstract theory became very real, very fast."

Friday · 13 March 2026

The Yellow Train Home

Checkout, a last morning along the canals, and the long train back to Bruges.

📷 Train station
(add photo)

Friday was bittersweet. After checking out, we spent a last morning by the canals, old sailing ships moored along the waterfront, the city quiet and grey in the March light. Then the train station, the yellow NS carriages, and the long ride south.

"Five days that started with a map search and ended with a city we genuinely didn't want to leave. Would we go back? Without question."

The ride back to Bruges gave us time to reflect on everything: the lectures, the people, the ideas, and a city that surprised us at every turn.
